Understanding Migraine as a Neurological Disorder

By Dr. Nisa Raisani
Consultant Neurologist & Medical Researcher

Published 2025 • Evidence-Based • Educational

Migraine is among the most prevalent neurological disorders worldwide, yet it remains widely misunderstood. Historically described as a vascular headache, migraine is now recognized as a disorder of brain function involving altered sensory processing and neural excitability.

Brain Excitability and Migraine

Neurophysiological studies demonstrate that individuals with migraine exhibit heightened cortical responsiveness to sensory stimuli. This abnormal excitability predisposes the brain to attacks triggered by internal or external factors.

Cortical Spreading Depression

Cortical spreading depression represents a wave of neuronal and glial depolarization that travels across the cerebral cortex. It is closely associated with migraine aura and contributes to changes in pain perception and cerebral blood flow.

The Trigeminovascular Pathway

Activation of the trigeminovascular system results in the release of neuropeptides, particularly calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP). CGRP plays a central role in migraine pain transmission, neurogenic inflammation, and central sensitization.

Clinical Relevance

Advances in understanding migraine mechanisms have led to targeted therapies, including CGRP antagonists and neuromodulation strategies. These treatments represent a shift from nonspecific pain control to mechanism-based management.

Implications for Medical Education

Recognizing migraine as a neurological disorder encourages evidence-based diagnosis and reduces stigma. For medical students, this framework supports rational treatment planning and patient-centered care.

Key Takeaways

  • Migraine is a disorder of brain excitability
  • CGRP is a key mediator in migraine pain
  • Modern therapies target specific neural mechanisms
